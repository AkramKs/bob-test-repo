What Are Monkeys?

Monkeys are haplorhine primates that make up the infraorder Simiiformes. They are divided into two major groups: New World monkeys (Platyrrhini), native to Central and South America, and Old World monkeys (Cercopithecidae), found across Africa and Asia. With over 260 living species, monkeys inhabit tropical rainforests, savannas, mountainous regions, and even urban areas.

Most monkeys are arboreal (tree-dwelling) and possess forward-facing eyes, grasping hands and feet, and relatively large brains compared to other mammals. Their diets range from fruits, leaves, and flowers to insects, eggs, and small vertebrates.

Famous Monkey Species

  • Mandrill (Mandrillus sphinx) — the largest monkey species, known for its vividly colored face and rump.
  • Japanese Macaque (Macaca fuscata) — also called "snow monkeys," famous for bathing in hot springs during winter.
  • Spider Monkey (Ateles) — a New World monkey with incredibly long limbs and a prehensile tail used as a fifth hand.
  • Proboscis Monkey (Nasalis larvatus) — recognizable by its large, fleshy nose; an excellent swimmer native to Borneo.
  • Golden Lion Tamarin (Leontopithecus rosalia) — a small, striking orange-gold monkey from Brazil's Atlantic coastal forests.
  • Capuchin Monkey (Cebus) — highly intelligent tool-users often seen in research and films.
  • Howler Monkey (Alouatta) — named for its incredibly loud vocalizations that can travel over three miles through dense forest.

🐒 Fun Monkey Facts

  • Monkeys use grooming not just for hygiene but to build social bonds and reduce group tension.
  • The pygmy marmoset is the world's smallest monkey, weighing only about 100 grams — roughly the weight of a deck of cards.
  • Capuchin monkeys have been observed using stone tools to crack open nuts, a behavior passed down through generations.
  • Unlike apes, most monkeys have tails — and many New World monkeys have prehensile tails that act like an extra limb.
  • A group of monkeys is called a troop, which can range from a handful to over a hundred individuals.
  • Monkeys appear in the mythology and folklore of cultures worldwide, from the Hindu god Hanuman to the Chinese Monkey King (Sun Wukong).

Monkeys vs. Apes — What's the Difference?

People often confuse monkeys with apes, but there are clear distinctions. Apes (chimpanzees, gorillas, orangutans, gibbons, and humans) lack external tails, have more complex brains, and possess a more upright body posture. Monkeys generally have tails, smaller body-to-brain ratios, and a more horizontal, quadrupedal walking style. If it has a tail, it's a monkey — not an ape!
